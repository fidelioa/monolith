Tuberculosis Applied Epidemiology

Fast Track Clinical Science MBBS - 3rd Year | Communicable Diseases

Introduction

Tuberculosis is an infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which mainly affects lungs. It can also affect intestine, meninges, bones and joints, lymph glands, skin and other tissues of the body.

WHO Definitions of TB Cases

Recommended for use since March 2013 and utilized by Nepal since 2015:

  1. Presumptive Case: Refers to a patient who presents with symptoms or signs suggestive of TB (previously known as TB suspect).
  2. Bacteriologically Confirmed Case: A patient from whom a biological specimen is positive by smear microscopy, culture or WHO-approved rapid diagnostic test (such as Xpert MTB/RIF). All cases must be notified, regardless of whether TB treatment has started.
  3. Clinically Diagnosed Case: A patient who does not fulfill the criteria for bacteriologically confirmed TB but has been diagnosed with active TB by a clinician or other medical practitioner who has decided to give the patient a full course of TB treatment diagnosed by X-ray abnormality or histology.

Bacteriologically confirmed or clinically diagnosed cases of TB are also classified according to:

  • Anatomical site of disease
  • History of previous treatment
  • Drug resistance
  • HIV status

Classification of TB Cases

1. Based on Anatomical Site

  • Case of Pulmonary TB: Any bacteriologically confirmed or clinically diagnosed case of TB involving the lung parenchyma or tracheobronchial tree. Miliary TB is also a pulmonary TB due to the presence of lesions in the lung.
  • Case of Extra-pulmonary TB: Any bacteriologically confirmed or clinically diagnosed case of TB involving organs other than lungs, e.g. abdomen, genitourinary tract, joints and bones, lymph nodes, meninges, pleura and skin. Note: Tuberculous intra-thoracic lymphadenopathy (mediastinal and/or hilar) and tuberculous pleural effusion without radiological abnormalities in the lungs are also kept under extra-pulmonary TB.

2. Based on History of Previous TB Treatment

  • New case of TB: A patient who has never been treated for TB or has taken anti-TB drugs for less than one month.
  • Retreatment case of TB: A patient who has been treated for one month or more with anti-TB drugs in the past. Retreatment cases are further classified into:
    • Relapse: Patients previously treated, declared cured or treatment completed at the end of their most recent course, and now diagnosed with a recurrent episode of TB (true relapse or reinfection).
    • Treatment failure: Patients whose most recent course of treatment failed, i.e., they had a positive sputum smear or culture result at month 5 or later during treatment.
    • Treatment after loss to follow-up: Patients previously treated and declared "Lost to follow-up" at the end of their most recent course of treatment (previously termed: Return after default).
    • Other previously treated: Patients previously treated but whose outcome after their most recent course of treatment is unknown or undocumented.

3. Based on Drug Resistance

Type of Resistance Definition
Mono-resistance Resistance to one first-line anti-TB drug only.
Rifampicin Resistance (RR-TB) Resistance to rifampicin detected using phenotypic or genotypic methods, with or without resistance to other anti-TB drugs. Includes any resistance to rifampicin, whether mono-resistance, multidrug resistance, poly-drug resistance or extensive drug resistance.
Isoniazid Resistance (Hr-TB) Refers to Mycobacterium tuberculosis strains in which resistance to isoniazid and susceptibility to rifampicin has been confirmed in vitro.
Multi Drug Resistance (MDR-TB) Resistance to at least both isoniazid and rifampicin.
Extensive Drug Resistance (XDR-TB) Resistance to any fluoroquinolone and to at least one of three second-line injectable drugs (capreomycin, kanamycin and amikacin), in addition to multidrug resistance.

4. Based on HIV Status

  • HIV-positive TB patients
  • HIV-negative TB patients
  • HIV status unknown TB patients

Problem Statement

Global Burden

  • It is second only to HIV/AIDS as the greatest killer worldwide due to a single infectious agent. In 2018, 10 million people fell ill with tuberculosis and 1.2 million died from the disease.
  • 0.45% of all TB cases occurred in Nepal.
  • Over 95% of tuberculosis deaths occur in low and middle income countries and it is among the top five causes of death for women aged 5 to 44 years.
  • In 2019, an estimated 1 million children and 1.2 million people living with HIV/AIDS became ill with tuberculosis.
  • It is a leading killer of HIV-positive children and people, causing one-fourth of all HIV-related deaths. In 2015, 0.4 million people died of HIV-associated tuberculosis.
  • In 2019, an estimated 208,000 people developed multidrug-resistant tuberculosis and an additional 100,000 people with rifampicin-resistant TB.
  • A 9% reduction in deaths occurred from 2015–2019. An estimated 60 million lives were saved through tuberculosis diagnosis and treatment between 2000 and 2018.

TB Burden of Nepal FY 2081/82 (Source: DoHS Annual Health Report 2081/82)

A total of 98.6% of all notified TB cases were incident cases (new and relapse). 72.4% of all notified TB cases were pulmonary TB, of which 58.3% were bacteriologically confirmed. Epidemiology

a. Epidemiological Indices

  1. Prevalence of infection: The percentage of population who show a positive reaction to the standard tuberculin test.
  2. Incidence of infection: The percentage of population under study who will be newly affected by Mycobacterium tuberculosis among the non-infected of the preceding survey during the course of one year. It reflects the annual risk of being infected.
  3. Prevalence of disease (case rate): The percentage of individuals whose sputum is positive for tubercle bacilli on microscopic examination. It is the best available practical index to estimate the number of infectious cases or case load in a community.
  4. Incidence of new cases: The percentage of new tuberculosis cases (confirmed by bacteriological examination) per 1,000 population occurring during one year.
  5. Prevalence of 'suspect' cases: Based on x-ray examination of chest.
  6. Case detection rate: Calculated as the number of notifications of new and relapse cases in a year divided by the estimated incidence of such cases in the same year.
  7. Prevalence of drug-resistant cases: The prevalence of patients excreting tubercle bacilli resistant to anti-tuberculosis drugs.
  8. Mortality rate: The number of deaths from tuberculosis every year per 1,000 population.

b. Agent Factors

i. Agent Characteristics:

  • Causative organism: Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
  • Discovered by Robert Koch in 1882.
  • Non-motile, non-sporing, and non-capsulated bacillus.
  • Measures 2–3 × 0.2–0.4 μm.
  • Arranged singly or in groups.
  • Acid-fast bacillus due to mycolic acid in the cell wall.
  • Weakly Gram-positive.
  • On Ziehl-Neelsen stain: slender, straight or slightly curved, beaded rods.

ii. Source of Infection:

  • Human source (most common):
    • Untreated or inadequately treated sputum-positive pulmonary TB patient.
    • One infectious case infects about 10–15 persons/year.
  • Bovine source:
    • Usually acquired through infected milk.

c. Host & Socio-Economic Factors

  • Age & Sex:
    • Mainly affects the productive age group (15–55 years).
    • More common in young people in developing countries.
    • More common in the elderly in developed countries.
    • More prevalent in males.
  • Immunity & Vulnerabilities:
    • No inherited immunity.
    • Immunity acquired by natural infection or BCG vaccination.
    • Inherited susceptibility is a risk factor.
    • Predisposing factors:
      • Malnutrition
      • Alcoholism
      • Diabetes mellitus
      • HIV infection
  • Socio-Economic Factors:
    • Poor quality of life.
    • Poor housing.
    • Overcrowding.
    • Population explosion.
    • Undernutrition.
    • Lack of education.
    • Large families.
    • Early marriages.
    • Lack of awareness of illness.

Clinical Review

  • Mode of Transmission:
    • Mainly by droplet infection and droplet nuclei from sputum-positive pulmonary TB patients.
    • Coughing generates the largest number of infectious droplets.
    • Droplet nuclei (1–10 μm) remain suspended in air for long periods.
    • Not transmitted through fomites (dishes or personal articles).
  • Incubation Period:
    • 3–6 weeks.
  • Clinical Features:
    • Fever.
    • Weight loss.
    • Hemoptysis.
    • Night sweats.
    • Persistent cough.
    • Fatigue.
    • Chest pain.

Control & Case Finding

Challenges in Control:

  • No completely effective vaccine.
  • Prolonged duration of treatment.
  • Delays in diagnosis and case tracking.
  • TB-HIV co-infection.

Control is achieved epidemiologically when:

  • Prevalence of natural infection in the 0–14 years age group falls to 1%.

Control Measures:

  • Eliminate the reservoir of infection.
  • Break the chain of transmission.
  • Protect susceptible individuals through BCG vaccination.
  • Early case detection and treatment.

Case Finding Tools

1. Sputum Examination

  • Method of choice for case finding because it is:
    • Reliable
    • Cheap
    • Easy to perform
    • Detects the most infectious pulmonary TB cases
  • Indications:
    • Cough >2 weeks
    • Chest pain
    • Hemoptysis
    • Low-grade fever of long duration
  • Collection:
    • Two sputum samples are collected.
    • First: Spot sample.
    • Second: Collected 1 hour later.
  • Microscopy:
    • Positive when ≥10,000 bacilli/ml of sputum.
    • One positive sample out of two is sufficient to diagnose smear-positive TB.
    • If both samples are negative, give symptomatic treatment and broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  • False Positives:
    • Scratches on slides retaining red stain.
    • Transfer of AFB from positive to negative slides.
    • Environmental mycobacterial contamination.
    • Acid-fast environmental particles.
  • False Negatives:
    • Inadequate sputum sample.
    • Collection/container errors.
    • Faulty smear preparation.
    • Interpretation errors.
    • Administrative cross-contamination.

2. Sputum Culture

  • Indicated when sputum smear is negative but TB is still suspected.
  • Used for drug sensitivity testing.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Difficult and tedious.
    • Takes >6 weeks.
    • Expensive.
    • Requires special expertise.

3. Mass Miniature Radiography & Tuberculin Skin Test

  • Chest X-ray:
    • Useful when sputum results are equivocal.
    • Mass screening is no longer recommended due to high cost and variable interpretation.
  • Tuberculin Skin Test (Mantoux Test):
    • Discovered by Von Pirquet (1907).
    • Indicates past or present TB infection.
    • Limited value in diagnosing active TB disease.

The Mantoux Test Procedure

  • Inject 1 TU of PPD in 0.1 ml intradermally on the flexor surface of the forearm.
  • Read after 72 hours.
  • Measure the horizontal transverse diameter of induration only (not erythema) using a transparent ruler.
Induration (mm) Interpretation
>20 mm Strong reactor; high risk of active disease.
>10 mm Positive (infection present, not necessarily active disease).
6–9 mm Doubtful; may indicate present or past infection.
<6 mm Negative; not infected (candidate for BCG vaccination).
  • False Positives:
    • Faulty technique.
    • Too deep injection.
    • Non-tuberculous mycobacteria.
    • Previous BCG vaccination.
  • False Negatives:
    • Subcutaneous injection.
    • Improper antigen storage.
    • Severe TB disease.
    • HIV infection (CD4 <200 cells/mm³).
    • Renal failure.
    • Diabetes mellitus.
    • Extreme age.
    • Malnutrition.
    • Viral infections.
    • Immunosuppressive therapy.

Treatment & DOTS Framework

Chemotherapy is indicated in every active case, delivered free of charge. Adherence is critical to prevent relapse and drug resistance.

  • Bactericidal Drugs: Rifampicin (R), Isoniazid (H), Streptomycin (S), Pyrazinamide (Z).
  • Bacteriostatic Drugs: Ethambutol (E) (Contraindicated in children below 5 years; it is the only bacteriostatic first-line drug), Thioacetazone (T).

Directly Observed Treatment Short Course (DOTS)

A global management strategy ensuring cure by observing patients swallow medications during the intensive phase. The continuation phase utilizes multiblister combipacks checked via empty pack returns. Community-Based DOTS (CB-DOTS) brings care close to patients through volunteers to increase accessibility, adherence, and decrease social stigma.

The 5 Core Elements of DOTS:

  1. Political commitment with increased and sustained financing.
  2. Case detection through quality-assured bacteriology.
  3. Standardized treatment, with supervision and patient support.
  4. An effective drug supply and management system.
  5. Monitoring, evaluation system, and impact measurement.

Drug Resistant TB (MDR & XDR-TB)

MDR-TB is caused by bacteria resistant to at least Isoniazid and Rifampicin, developing due to human error (interrupted treatment, faulty prescriptions, drug supply failure). Nepal introduced MDR-TB case management in 2005. Primary resistance occurs in patients never exposed to the drug; secondary resistance develops during treatment due to non-compliance or poor supervision.

Xpert MTB/RIF: A cartridge-based DNA amplification tool used as an accurate marker for early diagnosis and tracking of Rifampicin resistance. Introduced in Nepal in 2011/2012 with expansion to multiple centers. DOTS Plus programs integrate diagnostic and treatment support mechanisms for managing drug resistance.

MDR-TB Regimen: Uses essential and second-line agents like Aminoglycosides, Fluoroquinolones, and new classes like Bedaquiline (BBQ) targeting mycobacterial ATP synthetase. Standard shorter regimen template: 6(9) Km Lfx Eto Cs ZE + (18) Lfx Eto Cs E H.

Extensively Drug-Resistant TB (XDR-TB): Resistant to Isoniazid, Rifampicin, any Fluoroquinolone, and at least one of three second-line injectables (amikacin, kanamycin, or capreomycin). Documented in roughly 10% of MDR-TB cases.

DR-TB Current Status in Nepal (FY 2079/80)

An estimated 2,900 people had MDR/RR-TB, but only 693 were detected (representing a 76% detection gap). Out of 546 cases who received DR treatment: 49% were MDR Shorter Term Regimen (SSTR), 35% were MDR Longer Term Regimen (LTR), 15% were Pre-XDR, and 1% were XDR. Lumbini province has the highest burden of Pre-XDR and XDR cases. The current MDR treatment success rate stands at 82%.

BCG Vaccination

Developed by Calmette and Guerin by attenuating a bovine strain after 230 subcultures. It induces a benign artificial primary infection to stimulate immunity and prevent severe complications like tuberculous meningitis and miliary TB.

  • Vaccine Details: Live bacterial vaccine using the 'Danish 1331' strain. Freeze-dried form is highly stable and preferred. Efficacy ranges from 0-80% with protection lasting 15-20 years.
  • Storage: Maintained at 2–8°C in amber-colored bottles to prevent light penetration from destroying efficacy.
  • Administration: Dose is 0.05 ml at birth (0.1 ml for adults) injected intradermally using a tuberculin/omega microstat syringe at the insertion of the deltoid muscle (Right arm in Nepal). Reconstituted with Normal Saline. Note: Never given IM or SC due to risks of local abscesses and regional suppurative lymphadenitis.
  • Evolution: Intradermal wheal → Macule → Papule (2-3 weeks) → Ulcer (4-8 mm in 5 weeks) → Healing with a permanent, tiny rounded scar within 6-12 weeks.
  • Contraindications: Generalized eczema, infective dermatitis, hypogammaglobulinemia, immunocompromised states (HIV, Leukemia, Lymphoma), immunosuppressive treatments, or pregnancy.

Special Clinical Conditions

Maternal & Newborn Protection

Transmission from a sputum-positive mother can occur transplacentally via the umbilical vein. Prevention requires prompt ATT for the mother (all first-line drugs except Streptomycin are safe). If the mother has been treated for <2 weeks or is sputum-positive, the newborn receives Isoniazid prophylaxis. If the newborn's Mantoux test is negative, prophylaxis is not required. Breastfeeding is recommended under all circumstances by the WHO, but direct close contact should be minimized, and barrier nursing/isolation used if the mother has drug-resistant disease.

HIV and TB Co-Infection

HIV damages the immune system and accelerates the progression of latent TB to active, life-threatening disease, increasing the risk 30-fold. In Nepal, HIV testing among TB patients rose to 89% in FY 2079/80, with an ART enrollment of 99%. The co-morbidity rate is 0.6% among those tested.

The "3 I's" for HIV/TB Control: Intensified case finding, Isoniazid preventive therapy, and Infection control. Management requires starting TB treatment and Cotrimoxazole Preventative Therapy (CPT) first, followed by ART 2 to 8 weeks later to avoid adverse complications.

National TB Control Program & Strategic Plan

The National Strategic Plan to End TB (2021/22-2025/26) aligns with global targets. Nepal demonstrated a 15.0% reduction in TB incidence and a 1.9% reduction in TB deaths between 2015 and 2022, alongside a 3.0% decline in annual incidence rates based on prevalence surveys.

  • 2025/26 Goals: Decrease the incidence rate from 238 to 181 per 100,000 population; decrease the mortality rate from 58 to 23 per 100,000 population; reduce catastrophic costs for affected families to zero.
  • Long-term Goals: End the TB epidemic by 2091/92 (2035) and achieve full elimination by 2107 (2050).

The End TB Strategy Pillars (WHO)

  1. Integrated, Patient-Centered Care and Prevention: Early diagnosis, universal drug-susceptibility testing, systematic screening of high-risk contacts, treatment of all forms of TB, co-morbidity management, and preventive vaccinations.
  2. Bold Policies and Supportive Systems: Political commitment, community and civil society engagement, universal health coverage regulatory frameworks, mandatory case notifications, social protection, and poverty alleviation.
  3. Intensified Research and Innovation: Discovery, development, rapid uptake of new tools, and optimized implementation tracking.

Major Diagnostic Expansion (FY 2079/80 updates)

GeneXpert laboratories expanded to 113 sites, and Xpert XDR testing was introduced across 19 facilities. GeneXpert Ultra testing was launched alongside stool sample testing for pediatric diagnostics. Shorter oral Bedaquiline regimens were deployed for MDR-TB. Biometric identification systems were introduced at treatment sites, and interoperability was established between NTPMIS and HMIS platforms. Field operations for the National Anti-TB Drug Resistance Survey were completed, and protocols for the National Patient Cost Survey were drafted.
