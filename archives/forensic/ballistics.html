Forensic Ballistics & Explosion Injuries

Forensic Medicine — Fast Track Clinical Science - MBBS, 3rd Year

Introduction to Forensic Ballistics

Key Definitions

  • Firearm: Any instrument with which it is possible to propel a projectile (missile) by the expansive force of gases generated by the combustion of propellant (gun powder) in a closed space.
  • Ammunition: The material used to cause the explosion - i.e., the ingredients used such as bullet, pellet, powder, etc.
  • Ballistics: The science that deals with the investigation of firearms, ammunition, and the effects arising from their use.

Subdivisions of Forensic Ballistics

  1. Proximal (Internal) Ballistics: Includes the study of firearms and projectiles used inside the weapon.
  2. Intermediate (External) Ballistics: Includes the study of movement or motion of the projectile after it exits the firearm till the time it hits the target.
  3. Terminal (Wound) Ballistics: Includes the study of injuries produced by a firearm when the missile hits the target.
Mnemonic for Ballistics Types:
Remember the three types of ballistics as Internal → External → Terminal (IET):
• Inside → En-route → Target.

Classification of Firearms

A) According to Condition of Barrel:

  • Smooth bore firearm (e.g., shotgun)
  • Rifled firearm (e.g., pistol, rifle)
  • Country made firearm
  • Air gun
  • Paradox gun

B) According to Muzzle Velocity:

  • Low velocity: Up to 1,200 ft/s (e.g., revolver, pistol)
  • Medium velocity: Between 1,200–2,500 ft/s
  • High velocity: >3,000 ft/s (e.g., machine gun)

Basic Structure of Firearm

In general, a firearm is composed of three main parts:

  1. Barrel: A hollow metallic tube whose inner lumen or cylinder is known as the bore. The front end is the muzzle end; the posterior (rear) end is the breech end.
  2. Grip or Butt (Stock): The part held in the hand or placed at the shoulder.
  3. Action: The firing mechanism consisting of the trigger, trigger guard, bolt, and striker or hammer.

Anatomy of a Handgun (Pistol) Components

  • Chamber: The posterior-most (rear) part of the barrel which accommodates the cartridge to be fired. The backside has a metal plate called the breech plate with a central hole. The front part tapers into the lead before continuing anteriorly as the barrel.
  • Magazine: The receptacle for cartridges in a repeating-type weapon, from which cartridges are fed automatically into the chamber by the action mechanism.
  • Trigger Guard: A locking mechanism designed to prevent accidental pull of the trigger.
  • Bolt: The part which incorporates the firing pin, spring, and trigger mechanism.
  • Striker or Hammer: Located at the posterior part of the chamber, equipped with a pointed firing pin and a spring.

Chemical Constituents

1. Igniting Powder (Detonator/Primer): Made up of Mercuric fulminate and Lead oxide.

2. Gun Powder (Propellant): Three main types:

  • Black Gunpowder: Charcoal (15%) + Sulphur (10%) + KNO3 (75%). Produces 200–300 ml of gas per grain. [Mnemonic: @CSK]
  • Smokeless Gunpowder: Mainly Nitrocellulose (may also contain nitroglycerine and nitroguanidine). Combustion does not produce smoke. Highly efficient, producing 800–900 ml of gas per grain.
  • Semismokeless Gunpowder: 80% black powder + 20% nitrocellulose.

Rifled Firearms & Rifling

In rifled firearms, bullets are fired which spin on their own longitudinal axis. Examples include pistols, revolvers, military rifles, and the AK-47.

Anatomy of Rifling

  • The inner side of the barrel is cut into spiral grooves longitudinally from the chamber to the muzzle end. This is called rifling.
  • Lands: The elevated areas inside the barrel wall.
  • Grooves: The depressed spiral areas cut into the barrel wall. These spiral patterns can be clockwise or anticlockwise.
  • Caliber of a Gun: The diameter of the barrel measured between two opposing lands of the rifling. For example, a 0.303 rifle has a caliber of 0.303 of an inch (usually given in inches or mm).
Medicolegal Note:
Rifling marks are completely unique per gun. As a bullet passes through the barrel, the lands and grooves scratch unique tool marks onto it. These act as the "fingerprint" of the gun, helping forensic scientists identify a specific crime weapon.

Advantages of Rifling

  1. Gives a spinning (gyroscopic) movement to the bullet along its long axis, providing steadiness and stability while traveling in air.
  2. Prevents wobbling or tumbling of the bullet in flight.
  3. Covers a significantly longer range.
  4. Ensures pinpoint accuracy of hitting the target.

Anatomy of a Rifled Firearm Cartridge

A cartridge consists of four primary parts:

  • Projectile / Bullet: Conical in shape, consisting of a Base, Body (Jacket + Lead Core), Cannelure, and a Nose/Tip (Ogive). Nose types are classified as: Round Nose, Square Nose, Pencil Point Nose, Flat Point, or Long Round Nose.
  • Cartridge Case: An elongated cylindrical case made of brass (copper-zinc alloy) that binds the bullet, holds the propellant, and contains the percussion cap at its base.
  • Propellant: Gunpowder packed inside the cartridge case.
  • Detonator / Primer: Located in the percussion cap at the rim/head of the cartridge base.

Types of Bullets & Phenomena

  • Jacketed Bullet: Features a metallic cover made of steel, copper, nickel, or zinc. A Full Metal Jacket (FMJ) covers all parts except the base; a Semi-jacket leaves the nose uncovered.
  • Mushroom Bullet: A semi-jacketed bullet designed to expand outwards like a mushroom upon impact due to its soft, uncovered nose.
  • Dum-Dum Bullet: A jacketed bullet (.303 centerfire rifle) where an area near the nose is left uncovered to expose the core. Upon striking the body, it expands aggressively and produces a massive, destructive exit hole.
  • Tandem Bullet (Piggyback Bullet): Two bullets ejected one after another during a single trigger pull. This occurs when a first bullet fails to leave the barrel due to low pressure, and a subsequently fired bullet strikes it and forces both out together. Cause: Faulty ammunition or a loaded weapon left unused for years.
  • Duplex Bullet: Intentionally contains two projectiles by design; used in military applications to strike a target at different points simultaneously.
  • Frangible Bullet: Designed to shatter and fragment completely upon impact to prevent over-penetration or ricochet.
  • Souvenir Bullet: A bullet that has been present in the body for a long period without causing active bleeding. It becomes encapsulated by dense fibrous tissue. They are often left inside if retrieval would cause more harm (e.g., located in the spine).
  • Incendiary Bullet: Contains chemical components like phosphorus that catch fire on hitting the target, used by militaries to ignite fuel or structures.
  • Tracer Bullet: Contains a pyrotechnic composition that burns during flight, leaving a visible trail or 'trace' so the gunner can track the path of the shot.
  • Rubber Bullet: Typically non-lethal, rubber-coated projectiles fired from specialized riot guns to disperse protests.
  • Poisoned Bullet: Small caliber bullets (usually 0.177) chemically treated or packed with deadly toxins such as curare, ricin, or aflatoxin.
  • Yawing Bullet: A bullet traveling in an irregular, unstable fashion (fishtailing) instead of flying perfectly nose-on.
  • Tumbling Bullet: An unstable bullet that completely rotates end-over-end along its flight path.
  • Ricochet Bullet: A bullet that strikes an intervening object or surface before hitting the intended target, rebounds at an angle, and then goes on to strike another object. Round nose, full-metal jacketed, and low-velocity bullets are more prone to ricochet.
  • Bullet Graze: When a bullet just licks or scrapes across the skin surface and flies off without actually entering the body.
The Kennedy Phenomenon:
Named after the assassination of US President John F. Kennedy. When a surgical intervention or medical procedure is performed directly through a firearm wound in a life-saving attempt, it distorts and alters the original wound features. Later during autopsy, this can cause immense confusion as the entry wound no longer resembles a classic firearm defect until the medical intervention details are cleared up.

Smooth Bore Firearms (Shotguns)

  • Definition: Firearms whose inner barrel surface is entirely smooth, without any lands or grooves. They are designed to fire multiple spherical lead pellets at a single time.
  • Choking: A mechanical narrowing or constriction of the barrel at the muzzle end of a shotgun.

Advantages of Choking

  1. Keeps the cloud of lead pellets compact and prevents their early dispersion over long distances.
  2. Causes multiple pellets to act like a single solid ball for a longer duration, exerting a massive localized impact.
  3. Exerts a momentary obstruction to the expanding gases, building up increased internal pressure. Consequently, pellets leave the muzzle with greater velocity, extending the effective range and depth of penetration.

Degrees of Choke Constriction: Full choke (40/1000th of an inch) • 3/4 choke (30/1000) • 1/2 choke (20/1000) • 1/4 choke (10/1000).

Measurement of Gun Bore

  • Direct Method: Measuring the internal diameter in decimals of an inch. E.g., a 0.410 shotgun means the inner diameter is exactly 0.410 of an inch.
  • Indirect Method (Gauge System): The bore number is determined by the number of pure spherical lead balls fitting exactly inside the barrel that can be cast from 1 pound (454 g) of lead. For example, a 12-bore shotgun means that 12 identical lead spheres can be made from 1 pound of lead, and each sphere fits perfectly in the bore. Therefore, as the bore number increases, the barrel diameter decreases (e.g., a 4-bore is massive compared to a 32-bore).

Anatomy of a Shotgun Cartridge

Unlike rifled cartridges, a smooth bore shell contains: a outer plastic/cardboard case closed via a star crimp or cardboard disc at the front, filled with multiple lead pellets, separated by a thick compressed disc called a wad from the propellant, resting on a brass head containing the percussion cap.

The Function of a Wad:
The wad acts like a piston and creates a tight gas seal within the smooth bore. It prevents the expanding high-pressure gases from escaping past or blowing through the pellets, ensuring maximum kinetic energy transfer.

Wound Ballistics & Rifled Wounds

Key Firearm Injury Signatures

Feature Causative Mechanism & Forensic Description
Singeing of Hair Grossly swollen, curly, fragile, blackish hair; emits a peculiar odor due to the burning of keratin by the flash of flame from the muzzle.
Blackening Soot deposition due to smoke produced from the burning of gun powder. Can be wiped away.
Tattooing / Stippling The permanent embedding of unburnt or partially burnt gunpowder particles into the skin layer. Cannot be wiped away.
Cherry Red Tissue Carbon monoxide (CO) gas evolved during gunpowder combustion binds with tissue hemoglobin, creating a bright cherry-red color in tissues beneath the wound.
Abrasion Collar A circular zone of scraped skin surrounding the entry wound, produced by friction from the spinning action and entry of the bullet stretching the elastic skin.
Grease Collar / Dirt Ring A dark rim deposited right at the edge of the entry wound, caused by the bullet wiping off lubricant, grease, or fouling picked up from the gun barrel.
Why is the Entry Wound Smaller Than the Bullet?
During entry, the highly elastic skin is violently stretched and pushed inward by the bullet tip. After the bullet passes through, the elastic fibers snap back and retract, making the final visible hole appear smaller than the actual diameter of the bullet.

Rifled Firearm Wounds at Different Ranges

  • Contact Wound: The muzzle is held firmly against the skin. Gases, flame, smoke, and bullet enter together. The expanding gases accumulate between skin and bone, tearing the skin into a stellate, triangular, or cruciate star-shape (blast effect). An imprint abrasion of the muzzle face is stamped onto the skin. Cherry red tissues are present inside, and tattooing/blackening are minimal on the outside because everything is driven deep into the track.
  • Close Shot (2.5 to 7.5 cm): The victim is within range of the muzzle flame flash. Singeing of hair, prominent soot blackening, and dense tattooing are all present. The entry wound size matches the bullet diameter.
  • Near Shot (Up to 60 cm): The victim is beyond the flame reach but within range of gunpowder residue. Singeing and scorching are absent. Symmetrical tattooing and blackening are prominent around a clean, circular entry wound.
  • Distant Shot (Beyond 60–90 cm): The patient is entirely out of range of flame, smoke, and gunpowder residue. Scorching, singeing, blackening, and tattooing are completely absent. Only a clean-cut, circular entry wound with an abrasion collar and grease collar is visible.

Forensic Terms & Cranial Features:
• Back Spatter: The muzzle blast and sudden negative pressure inside the barrel can suck blood, hair, tissue fragments, and clothing fibers back into the gun barrel.
• Cranial Entry: A bullet hitting the skull leaves a clean, punched-in circular hole in the outer bone layer, but creates a wide, beveled, cone-like crater on the inner bone table.
• Puppe's Rule: When multiple fracture lines develop in the skull from successive gunshots, newer fracture lines will always stop abruptly when they cross a pre-existing fracture line from an earlier shot. This determines the exact sequence of firing.

Smooth Bore Firearm Injuries (Shotgun)

When a shotgun is fired, multiple pellets are released simultaneously. Their dispersion forms a cone-like cloud that increases dynamically with the distance traveled, while their velocity decreases rapidly.

Shotgun Range Characteristics

  • Contact Range: The muzzle is in firm contact. Circular or stellate wound with blackened, inverted edges. Pellets enter together as a tight, destructive mass (en masse). The wad and cardboard discs are driven deep inside. Muzzle imprint and cherry-red tissue discoloration are present.
  • Close Range (<15 cm): Single, large, irregular circular wound. Singeing of hair (up to 30 cm) and intense soot blackening (up to 50 cm) are prominent. Pellets are still found en masse along with the wad.
  • Short Range (15 cm to 1 meter): The single hole persists up to 1 meter, but the edges become jagged, scalloped, and chewed-up due to individual pellets beginning to separate at the fringes. This is classically known as a rat hole or rat nibbling appearance. Tattooing is present.
  • Medium Range (1 meter to 4 meters): Blackening, tattooing, and singeing are entirely absent. The main central hole shrinks or disappears, replaced by a wide spread of multiple independent pellet entry points. Small satellite wounds appear clustered tightly around the central defect. The wad may strike the skin independently, causing isolated abrasions or bruising up to 2–5 meters.
  • Distant Range (Beyond 4 meters): The central wound disappears entirely. The injury presents strictly as a wide, scattered pattern of dozens of independent, small, circular pellet entries covering a large surface area. No smoke, flame, wad marks, or tattooing can be found.

Explosion & Blast Injuries

A bomb is a container filled with an explosive mixture and fragments, initiated by a detonator or fuse. A blast injury is a complex physical trauma resulting from exposure to an explosion.

Classification of Explosives

  • High-order Explosives (HEs): Undergo ultra-rapid detonation, producing an instantaneous, supersonic blast wave under extremely high pressure. This causes devastating primary tissue tearing. Examples: TNT, dynamite, C-4, and ammonium nitrate.
  • Low-order Explosives (LEs): Undergo slower deflagration (burning) rather than detonation. They completely lack a supersonic shock wave. They are composed of propellants and pyrotechnics. Examples: Black powder, fireworks, and petroleum-based mixtures like Molotov cocktails.

Categorization of Blast Injuries

  1. Primary Injuries: Caused directly by the high-pressure blast wave compression. They are unique because external visible injuries are often completely absent, masking life-threatening internal hollow organ damage.
    • Acoustic Barotrauma: Rupture of the tympanic membrane, dislocation of middle ear ossicles, or inner ear disruption leading to permanent deafness.
    • Blast Lung: Severe compression tears the alveolar-capillary membrane, leading to capillary leakage, severe bilateral hemorrhage, thrombosis, and DIC. This is the most common cause of death among initial blast survivors. Presents with a clinical triad of dyspnea, bradycardia, and hypotension. Chest X-rays show a classic 'butterfly' pattern of fluffy infiltrates spreading from the hilum.
    • Gastrointestinal Tract: Perforation of gas-filled organs (colon, stomach, cecum), which is far more severe in underwater explosions.
    • Brain Blast: Diffuse axonal injury and concussion without a direct blow, resulting in a post-blast triad of headache, fatigue, anxiety, and memory loss.
  2. Secondary Injuries: Caused by flying shrapnel, nails, screws, debris, and fragments accelerated by the blast wind. This accounts for the majority of actual casualties.
    Marshall's Triad: Secondary injuries are characterized by the simultaneous presentation of Abrasions, Bruises, and Lacerations all at once across the body due to shrapnel impacts.
  3. Tertiary Injuries: Caused when the massive blast wind physically lifts and throws the victim's body against solid structures like walls or pavements. Results in severe fractures, blunt trauma, and coup-contrecoup brain injuries. Children are at higher risk due to lower body mass.
  4. Quaternary Injuries: Miscellaneous injuries including flash burns, building collapse crush injuries, toxic gas or smoke inhalation, and radiation exposure. Psychiatric trauma (PTSD) is incredibly prevalent, affecting even completely uninjured survivors.

Medicolegal Considerations

During a firearm or blast autopsy, the forensic pathologist must determine the type of weapon, the exact range of firing, the primary cause of death, and the manner of death (Accidental, Suicidal, or Homicidal).

Summary Differences: Rifled vs. Smooth Bore

Feature Rifled Firearms (Guns/Pistols) Smooth Bore Firearms (Shotguns)
Barrel Feature Rifling: Spiral longitudinal grooves cut inside the entire barrel length. Choking: Entirely smooth barrel with a mechanical constriction at the muzzle.
Projectile Type Fires a single, streamlined solid conical bullet. Fires a cloud of multiple, small, spherical lead pellets.
Missile Dynamics Induces gyroscopic spin along its long axis, ensuring flight stability. Pellets disperse progressively into a wider cone the further they travel.
Internal Parts Bullet, cartridge case, powder, primer. Pellets, gas-sealing wad, cardboard discs, powder, primer.
Injury Profile Leaves a single entry wound with a distinctive abrasion/grease collar. Ranges from a single rat hole entry to dozens of scattered pellet entry points.
