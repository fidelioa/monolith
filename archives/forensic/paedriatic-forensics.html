Terminology Frameworks

  • Infanticide: Unlawful destruction of a child under the age of one year. It is said to be committed at the time of, or within a few minutes or hours after, birth. It excludes death during labour when the fetus is destroyed via craniotomy or decapitation.
  • Foeticide: Killing of the fetus at any time prior to birth.
  • Filicide: The deliberate act of killing of a child by the parent (from Latin filius: son).
  • Neonaticide: Deliberate killing of a child within 4 weeks of its birth.

Still Birth vs. Dead Birth & Intrauterine Autolysis

Still Birth

A still born fetus is born after 28 weeks of pregnancy (reduced to 24 weeks in UK) and shows no sign of life after complete birth. The child was alive in utero but dies during the process of birth.

  • Risk Factors: Occurs more frequently among illegitimate, immature male children of primipara mothers.
  • Signs of Prolonged Labour: Edema and bleeding into the scalp, caput succedaneum, cephalhaematoma, and severe moulding of the head.
  • Causes: Prematurity, anoxia of various types, birth trauma (such as intracranial hemorrhage d/t excessive moulding), placental abnormalities, toxemia of pregnancy, erythroblastosis fetalis, and congenital defects.
Crucial Viva Signatures: Squames or meconium within alveoli do NOT indicate still birth; meconium staining of the body merely flags fetal distress. Medico-legally, a charge of infanticide will fail if the baby is proven stillborn and was not alive when fully born.

Dead Birth

A dead born child has died entirely in utero (at any intra-uterine age) and presents specific postmortem features immediately upon delivery.

  • Signs of Dead Birth: Rigor mortis at delivery, maceration, mummification, Robert's sign, hyperextension of the spine, and crowding of the ribs shadow with loss of parallelism.

Maceration (Aseptic Autolysis)

Maceration is a process of aseptic autolysis. It occurs when a dead child remains in the uterus for about 3 or 4 days surrounded by liquor amnii with the absolute exclusion of air, which prevents putrefaction from taking place.

10 Diagnostic Features of Maceration:
  1. Earliest Sign: Reddening of the skin with peeling and slippage, which can be seen as early as 12 hours after death in utero. The skin color becomes brown or purplish.
  2. Consistency: The body of a macerated foetus is soft, flaccid, and completely flattens out when placed on a level surface. It emits a sweetish, disagreeable odour.
  3. Blebs: Large blebs appear at 24 hours containing a red serous or serosanguineous fluid.
  4. Epidermis: The epidermis detaches easily, leaving moist and greasy areas with reddish tissue beneath due to hemolysis and edema.
  5. Abdomen: The abdomen becomes distended, and the serous cavity may contain a turbid reddish fluid after 48 hours.
  6. Joints & Bones: Joints are abnormally mobile. Bones are easily detachable and flexible from the soft parts.
  7. Viscera: All viscera become soft and edematous, losing their distinct morphology. However, the uterus and lungs remain unchanged for a long time.
  8. Umbilical Cord: The umbilical cord appears red, smooth, thickened, and soft.
  9. Skull & Brain: The skull bones are completely separated, and the brain has a greyish-red, pulpy appearance.
  10. Spine: Vertical collapse of the vertebral column occurs.
Spalding's Sign: This is a pathognomonic sign of intrauterine death. It refers to the loss of alignment and overlapping of fetal skull bones visible on X-ray. It occurs due to the liquefaction of the cerebrum and the softening of the ligamentous structures supporting the cranial vault. It typically appears about 7 days after death.

Mummification & Radiographic Findings

  • Mummification: Occurs when the fetus dies from a deficient blood supply, particularly when the liquor amnii is scanty and when no air enters the uterus.
  • Robert's Sign: The appearance of a gas shadow in the chambers of the heart and great vessels by 12 hours, occurring probably due to red blood cell disintegration and lysis.
  • Other Skeletal Transformations: Hyperextension of the spine and crowding of the ribs shadow with an absolute loss of parallelism.

Age Estimation & Fetal Metrics

  • Up to 5 Months (Hasse's Rule):√Height in cm = Age in months
  • After 5 Months (Hasse's Rule):(Length in cm) / 5 = Age in months
  • Centres of Ossification: At the 7th month, the centre of ossification appears on the lower end of the thigh bone (femur) and the upper part of the tibia. Other key sites are the foot bones (cuboid) and the sternum. While length is a reliable indication of maturity, the presence or absence of ossification centres provides more reliable evidence since small children as well as large can be born mature.

Live Birth & Establishment of Respiration

Live birth requires the child to show signs of life when even only a part of the child is out of the mother, regardless of whether it has breathed yet or is completely born. Causing its death is legally categorized as homicide or infanticide. In civil cases, circumstantial signs of life include crying, moving, or opening of eyes.

Despite these signs of life, a child may still die from natural causes before it is completely born. Therefore, proof of breathing as indicated by crying is not absolute legal proof of live birth. Law presumes that every newborn child found dead was born dead until the contrary is proved and demonstrated by postmortem examination of the child.

Postmortem Evidence of Separate Existence

  • Breslau's Second Life Test (Stomach-Bowel Test): Air is swallowed into the stomach and intestines during respiration. When removed under double ligatures, they float in water if respiration has taken place; otherwise, they sink. The presence of milk in the stomach provides positive, irrefutable evidence of live birth. Note that air may be present in the stomach after decomposition, or in a stillborn child actively attempting to free the air passages of fluid obstruction.
  • Changes in Blood: Nucleated red blood cells usually disappear completely from the blood within 24 hours. Fetal hemoglobin (Hb) before birth stands at about 80-90% and rapidly decreases to 7-8% by the third month.
  • Meconium Clearance: Meconium is a green, viscid substance consisting of thickened bile and mucus. In a live birth, the large intestine becomes completely free of meconium within 24 hours after birth. It remains present in the intestine in breech presentations and hypoxia, and it may be completely expelled before birth in certain stillborn fetuses. Meconium stains are brownish-green, stiffen cloth, and have an acidic reaction.
  • Wredin's Test (Middle Ear): Before birth, the middle ear contains gelatinous embryonic connective tissue and no air. With respiration, the sphincter at the pharyngeal end of the Eustachian tube relaxes, and air replaces the gelatinous substance within a few hours up to five weeks.
  • Changes in Skin Color: Vernix caseosa coats the axilla, inguinal region, neck folds, and buttocks. Skin color transitions from bright red just after birth, to darker at 2-3 days, and brick red-yellow-normal by 1 week.
  • Umbilical Cord Vitality: The presence of marks of crushing by artery forceps, clean-cut margins, and a structural ligature provide strong suggestive evidence of a live birth.

The Hydrostatic Test (Fodere's Test)

Principle: Based on specific gravity shifts. Unrespired lung specific gravity sits at 1040–1050 (and thus sinks in water); post-respiration, it drops to approximately 940 due to tidal air entry, causing the respired lung to float.

Procedural Cascade

Tie double ligatures on the bronchi → Separate lungs from thoracic contents → Place individual intact lungs in water

*Control measure: A small piece of liver may serve as a control. If the liver floats, the test is invalid due to decomposition.

↓
If intact lungs float or sink → Cut each lung into 12–20 individual pieces → Test pieces in water
↓
If pieces float → Squeeze pieces firmly between thumb and index finger under the surface of the water
↓
Observe for escaping air dots → Wrap pieces in a cloth → Apply a heavy pressure weight to expel tidal air
↓
Final Flotation Check: If pieces still float = Respired lung. If they sink after pressure = Unrespired/Feeble respiration.

Fallacies & Diagnostic Exceptions

  • False Positives (Unrespired Lungs Float): Accumulation of decomposition gases due to putrefaction, artificial inflation of the lung via forceful mouth-to-mouth resuscitation efforts, or congenital pneumonitis of infective origin.
  • False Negatives (Respired Lungs Sink): Very feeble respiration for a very short timeframe involving only a tiny surface area, atelectasis, or severe pulmonary edema.
Hydrostatic Test is NOT Necessary If:
1. Fetus exhibits gross fatal congenital malformations incompatible with life (e.g., anencephaly, meaning the fetus is a monster).
2. Fetus is below 28 weeks gestational age (immature and non-viable).
3. Stomach contains milk or umbilical cord shows cicatrization/healing.
4. Fetus shows signs of intra-uterine maceration or mummification.
5. Bruises are evident on the lung surfaces, indicating violent active respiratory effort.

Maturity & Causes of Infant Death

Appearance of Full-Term Mature Infant

  • Metrics: Crown-heel length: 48-52 cm; Crown-rump length: 28-32 cm; Weight: 2500-3300 gm (female infants average about 100 gm less).
  • Features: Lanugo restricted strictly to the shoulders; head well covered with hair 2-3 cm long. Nails project past the fingertips and to the end of toes; nose and ear cartilage fully formed. Limbs and body are plump; face has completely lost its wrinkles. Pupillary membrane is entirely absent.
  • Internal Features: Both testicles descended into the scrotum or labia majora closing the vulva. Meconium present in the large intestine. Ossification centres present in the cuboid and upper end of the tibia.

Classification of Infant Death

Infant deaths are categorised into Natural Causes (immaturity, congenital disease, malformations, erythroblastosis fetalis, debility, neonatal infection) and Unnatural Causes, which split into Accidental (Perinatal vs Postnatal) and Criminal modes.

Criminal Modes of Infanticide

  • Acts of Commission: Positive acts utilizing mechanical violence or poisoning to cause death.
    • Suffocation: Smothering by pressing the face into a pillow or closing the nose and mouth with hands. The required force is so minimal that it often leaves no trauma. Overlaying, or forcing mud, rags, or cotton wool into the airway are common variants.
    • Strangulation: Ligature, manual throttling, or using the umbilical cord to simulate natural twisting around the neck.
    • Poisoning: Chemical execution indicative of planned termination.
    • Head Injury: Striking the infant against a wall or hard floor by holding the feet, compression/crushing between two hands, or use of a blunt weapon.
    • Burning & Drowning: Rare mechanisms, usually applied for criminal disposal and concealment of the body.
    • Fractures and Dislocation: Deliberate twisting of the neck causing cervical vertebrae dislocation.
    • Wounds: Cut throat, stabs, and incised injuries.
    • Pithing (Concealed Puncture): Piercing the anterior fontanelle with a fine needle to damage intracranial structures. The child dies later of induced meningitis, which mimics a natural infection and avoids suspicion.
  • Acts of Omission (Neglect): Criminal negligence where a woman fails to take ordinary precautions to save her child. This includes failure to provide assistance during labour (causing suffocation or head injury), failure to tie the cut cord (leading to fatal hemorrhage), failure to protect the infant from exposure to heat and cold, failure to clear air passages of amniotic fluid/mucus, and withholding food or immunization.
Precipitate Labour Forensic Defense:
Precipitate labour terminates suddenly and rapidly without warning, merging all three stages of labour into one. It usually occurs in multipara women having a roomy birth canal and a small fetus; it is rare in primipara. It often happens during the cleaning of the bowels, preceded by a sudden urge. Legally, the mother may claim the child died accidentally due to head injury/skull fracture with subdural hemorrhage from dropping onto a hard floor while standing, suffocated/inhaled dirty water from falling into a lavatory pan, or suffered hemorrhage from a torn cord.

Child Abuse & Battered Baby Syndrome

Child Abuse constitutes actions, behaviors, or inaction by an adult that harms or endangers a child. It manifests in 4 types: physical abuse, sexual abuse, emotional abuse, and neglect.

Risk Factor Profile

  • Child Factors: Age usually under 3 years, more common in males, single children, illegitimate or unwanted status, acts of disobedience, frequent crying, refusal to take food, and bed wetting.
  • Parent Factors: Young parents, low educational status, low IQ, aggressive impulsive personality, financial hardships, habitual alcohol/drug abuse, and parents who were themselves victims of battering during childhood. Parents typically present a vague history of accident that fails to correspond with physical findings and seek medical help very late.

Diagnostic and Physical Stigmata

"The skin and bones tell us a story which the child is either too young or too frightened to tell."

  • Skin and Facial Markings: Bruises of varying ages and healing stages. Gripping the chest forcefully leaves clustered coin-shaped bruises along the ribs called "six penny" bruises. Pinching creates butterfly-shaped lesions. Belts and straps leave patterned marks on the buttocks and thighs. Slaps cause bruising to the cheeks, mouth, and pinna. Blows to the mouth often tear the mucosa inside the upper lip and the frenulum.
  • Ocular Traumas: Black eyes, conjunctival hemorrhage, dislocation of the lens, retinal detachment, and retinal hemorrhage.
  • Skeletal Traumas (X-Ray Highlights): Multiple fractures across different anatomical sites. Large periosteal hematomas form because infant periosteum strips easily. Metaphyseal, epiphyseal, and spiral twisting limb fractures are common. Pond fractures of the flexible skull and sutural diastasis can occur.
  • Nobbing Fractures: Multiple rib fractures along the posterior angles of the ribs, produced by the assailant holding the child's chest with both hands and shaking violently. After 1–2 weeks, callus forms over the fractured sites; on X-rays, they appear like a string of beads.
  • Infantile Whiplash Syndrome: Violent shaking causes rotational acceleration forces, rupturing delicate bridging intracranial vessels to form subdural hematomas, accompanied by intraocular and retinal hemorrhages.
  • Visceral & Other Injuries: Abdominal trauma causing liver lacerations leading to blood in the abdominal cavity (hemoperitoneum), clean-cut small bowel injuries, rupture of the stomach, circular pitted cigarette burns, bite marks, traumatic alopecia (hair pulled in clumps), and broken fingers.
Medico-Legal Importance of BBS: This syndrome must be diagnosed when there is a clear variance between injury type and the history provided, when injuries of different ages are found simultaneously, and when there is a purposeful delay in seeking medical care. Offending caretakers can be charged with culpable homicide amounting or not amounting to murder.

Munchausen & Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy

  • Munchausen Syndrome: A condition where an adult compulsively, consciously, and driven by internal psychology feigns, exaggerates, or actively self-induces illness to undergo unnecessary hospital investigations and treatments across multiple institutions.
  • Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy (MSBP): A dangerous form of child abuse where a caretaker (typically the mother) fabricates or actively induces illness in her child to secure medical attention and satisfy a psychological need for sympathy and attention. Over 50% of these mothers possess a personal abnormal illness behavior history.

Rosenberg Diagnostic Criteria

  1. Illness produced, fabricated, or alleged entirely by a parent or guardian.
  2. Repeated requests for medical care resulting in multiple invasive, unnecessary procedures.
  3. Parental denial of any knowledge regarding the true etiology of the symptoms.
  4. Complete regression or disappearance of symptoms when the child is separated from the parent.

Methods of Simulation of Illness

  • Hematuria: The mother pricks her own finger and mixes blood into the child's urine sample.
  • Asphyxia/Apnea: Closing the child's nose with fingers and pushing the lower jaw up, or placing a pillow or towel over the face and pushing it down into bed clothing.
  • Hypoglycemia: Deliberate insulin administration to the child.
  • Gastrointestinal: Using Ipecacuanha to induce severe vomiting; laxatives or salt poisoning to trigger chronic diarrhea.
  • Neurological & Other: Dosing with theophylline to trigger convulsions, administering barbiturates to cause CNS depression, or scratching the skin to simulate chronic rashes.

Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS)

SIDS (Cot Death, Crib Death, Cradle Death) is the sudden and unexpected death of a seemingly healthy infant which remains completely unexplained even after a thorough autopsy, death scene investigation, clinical history review, and complete case evaluation.

Epidemiological Profile

  • Incidence & Profile: Occurs in 0.6 per 1,000 live births. It spans from 2 weeks to 2 years (most commonly 1–7 months, peaking sharply between 2 and 4 months) with a slight male predominance.
  • Risk Enhancers: Occurs almost exclusively during sleep periods. Risks are significantly higher in twins, premature infants, families with low socioeconomic status, and mothers who smoke cigarettes or abuse drugs during pregnancy.
  • Proposed Pathological Mechanisms: No single definite cause is known. Postulated mechanisms include prolonged sleep apnea cascades leading to hypoxia, bradycardia, and cardiac arrest; mild respiratory infections; nasal edema and mucus secretion; local hypersensitivity of the respiratory tract to cow's milk; cardiac conduction system defects; hypothyroidism; selenium deficiency; immunoreactivity anomalies in the hippocampus and medulla; metabolic disorders; hypothermia/hyperthermia; or accidental overlaying.

Autopsy Presentations (Diagnosis of Exclusion)

  1. External Findings: Milk- or blood-stained froth at the child's mouth, nostrils, or bedding. The infant's hands are frequently clenched tightly around bedcloth fibers. Petechial hemorrhages may spot the face and eyes.
  2. Internal Findings: The trachea contains milk vomit, sometimes blood-stained, containing shed epithelial cells. Prominent petechial hemorrhages appear over the thymus, lung surfaces, and the posterior epicardial surface of the heart.
  3. Pulmonary Status: Lungs are heavy, severely congested, firm, edematous, and exhibit patchy or uniform purplish discoloration of the surface with alveolar collapse and a measurable increase in weight.
Medico-Legal Importance of SIDS: SIDS is a natural death. A thorough postmortem is vital so that innocent parents are not wrongfully linked or prosecuted for criminal involvement, violence, or fatal negligence.
