Core Forensic Definitions

Injury

  • Medically: A break of continuity of any tissue of the body.
  • Legally: Any harm whatever illegally caused to any person in body, mind, reputation or property.

Wound

Defined as the break of the natural continuity of any of the tissues of the living body.

Assault vs. Battery

  • Assault: An offer of threat or attempt to apply force to the body of another in a hostile manner, regardless of whether physical injury occurs (e.g., shaking a fist or head hostilely).
  • Battery: The actual application of force to the body (e.g., beating or throwing an object), which does not strictly require direct body-to-body contact.

Hurt

The causing of bodily pain, disease, or infirmity (the inability of an organ to perform its usual function) to a person. It explicitly excludes mental pain.

Legal Framework: Simple vs. Grievous Hurt

Simple Hurt

An injury that is neither extensive nor serious, healing rapidly without leaving permanent deformity or disfiguration. Under Chapter 14, Section 191, Subsection 3 of the National Criminal Code, 2074 (2017), punishment includes imprisonment up to 3 years, a fine up to Rs 30,000, or both.

Grievous Hurt (अंग भंग)

An injury of a serious nature that does not heal rapidly and leaves permanent deformity or disfigurement. Under Subsection 4, punishment entails imprisonment up to 10 years and a fine up to Rs 1,00,000. If multiple organs share a function and only one is damaged, the penalty is halved; if it heals functionally with a scar, imprisonment is up to 3 years and a fine up to Rs 30,000.

Statutory Conditions of Grievous Hurt (Muluki Ain / Aparadh Samhita):
  1. Permanent privation of the sight of an eye (blindness).
  2. Permanent privation of the smell of the nose.
  3. Permanent privation of the hearing of an ear.
  4. Permanent privation of the speech of the tongue.
  5. Destruction or permanent impairment of a woman's breast.
  6. Emasculation/castration or permanent male impotence/loss of erection, or making a female sterile.
  7. Privation or non-functional status of any member or joint of the vertebra, pelvis, upper or lower limbs.
  8. Injury leading to permanent disability preventing occupational work.

Classification of Injuries

Injuries are classified on the basis of causative factor, legal significance, medico-legal nature, and temporal relation to death.

1. Based on Causative Factor

  • Mechanical Injuries
    • Blunt Force Injuries: Abrasion, Contusion, Laceration, Fracture and Dislocation.
    • Sharp Force Injuries: Incised wound, Chop wound, Stab wound.
    • Firearm Injuries: Firearm wounds and Blast injuries.
  • Thermal Injuries
    • Cold Injuries: Hypothermia, Frostbite, Trench foot.
    • Heat Injuries: Hyperpyrexia, Heat exhaustion, Heat cramps, Burns, Scalds.
  • Chemical Injuries
    • Corrosive Injuries: Caused by strong acids and strong alkalis.
    • Irritant Injuries: Caused by weak acids and weak alkalis.
  • Physical Injuries
    • Electrical injuries.
    • Radiation injuries (X-rays, ultraviolet rays, radioactive elements).
    • Lightning injuries.

2. Based on Legal Classification

  • Simple hurt.
  • Grievous hurt.

3. Based on Medico-legal Classification

  • Suicidal injuries.
  • Homicidal injuries.
  • Accidental injuries.
  • Defense wounds.
  • Fabricated (Artificial) wounds.

4. Based on Temporal Relation to Death

  • Antemortem wounds: Injuries sustained before death.
  • Postmortem wounds: Injuries produced after death.

Mechanical Injuries: Blunt Force

1. Abrasion

The removal of the superficial epithelial layer of skin by blunt force involving pressure and movement. They heal rapidly without scars unless extensive.

  • Scratch/Linear: Length with no significant width; narrow/sharp force. Leaves tissue tags at the end, pointing to direction (wide at start, narrow at end).
  • Graze: Broad, rough sliding forces producing parallel longitudinal lines. Highly common in road traffic accidents ("road rash"). Includes brush burns (dragging) and friction burns (tangential touch with smooth surfaces/clothing).
  • Pressure: Crushing of epidermis by perpendicular force with movement (e.g., ligature marks). Dries to a brown/black parchment-like look.
  • Imprint: Pure perpendicular impact leaving the exact patterned shape of the object (e.g., vehicle radiator marks).

2. Bruise / Contusion

Extravasation of blood into subcutaneous/subepithelial tissues due to capillary rupture by blunt force or pressure, without losing skin continuity.

  • Intradermal: Lies in immediate sub-epidermal layer; sharply defined patterned marks (whips, shoe soles).
  • Subcutaneous: Lies in the fatty layer with blurred edges; dark red swelling.
  • Deep: Deeper tissue bleeding; takes 1–2 days to appear ("delayed bruising").
  • Patterned: Reflects weapon shape. Includes Tram-line contusions (two parallel lines from a rod/stick) and six-penny bruises (fingertip pressure in strangulation).
  • Migratory / Ectopic: Blood shifts due to gravity and fascial planes. E.g., a blow to the forehead or an anterior cranial fossa fracture causes a Black Eye; a skull base fracture causes Battle's sign (behind ear) or Raccoon eyes.
Chronological Color Changes of a Bruise (Age Estimation):
• Fresh: Red (Fresh extravasation)
• 1–2 Days: Bluish (Deoxyhemoglobin)
• 4 Days: Bluish-black to Brown (Hemosiderin)
• 5–6 Days: Greenish (Hematoidin)
• 7–12 Days: Yellow (Bilirubin)
• 2 Weeks: Complete disappearance

3. Lacerated Wound

Splitting or tearing of tissue from hard, blunt impacts exceeding tissue elasticity.

  • Characteristics: Ragged, irregular edges, tissue bridges/tags spanning the gap, crushed hair bulbs, minimal bleeding due to crushed, retracted vessels, and the frequent presence of foreign matter (mud, glass).
  • Split Lacerations: Sandwiched tissue splits over bone. Can appear clean cut, mimicking an incision (Incised-looking lacerated wound over scalp, forehead, shin).
  • Avulsion: Tangential shearing force tearing off a portion of skin/viscus (flaying). Severe variants include traumatic amputation and decapitation.

Mechanical Injuries: Sharp Force

1. Incised Wound

A clean-cut wound caused by sharp edges, being longer than it is deep. Edges are clean, well-defined, everted, and free from abrasions or contusions. Profuse external bleeding occurs because vessels are cleanly divided. Shows tailing (shallower at the termination point).

2. Chop Wound

Inflicted by a heavy, sharp weapon (axe, meat cleaver) in a hacking motion. Margins are sharp but may show surrounding abrasion/bruising from weapon weight. The lower end (heel) of the blade strikes first and cuts deeper, indicating the position of the assailant.

3. Stab Wound

A penetrating trauma deeper than its surface length or width, caused by long, narrow, pointed weapons. Classified as penetrating (entry wound only into cavity), perforating (through-and-through entry and exit wounds), or punctured (terminates inside tissue outside a major cavity).

  • Hilt Mark: Full blade penetration causes the guard to strike the skin, leaving a patterned abrasion/bruising. Symmetrical at right angles; prominent above if a downward strike, below if an upward strike.
  • Langer's Cleavage Lines: Langer's cleavage lines are the natural lines of tension in the skin, corresponding to the predominant orientation of collagen fibers in the dermis. If the stab runs parallel to these skin lines, the wound remains a narrow slit. If it runs transversely, it gapes widely into a spindle shape.
  • Weapon Cross-Section Indicators: Double-edged blades leave an elliptical slit with two sharp angles. Single-edged blades leave a wedge/tear-drop shape with one blunt/squared-off end (which may show *fish-tailing* splits).

I. Incised vs. Lacerated vs. Stab Wounds

Feature Incised Wound Lacerated Wound Stab Wound
Weapon Sharp edge (knife, razor) Hard blunt object (stick, stone) Pointed instrument (dagger, needle)
Margins Smooth, clean cut, everted Irregular, ragged, undermined Clean cut, parallel edges
Surrounding No abrasion or bruising Abrasion and bruising common Rare (unless hilt mark hits)
Dimensions Longer than deep Irregular, longer than deep Depth is the greatest dimension
Tissue Bottom Cleanly cut completely Tissue bridges, intact nerves/vessels Cleanly cut completely
Hemorrhage Profuse and external Slight (vessels crushed/retracted) Variable; heavily internal
Hair Bulbs Cleanly cut Crushed or torn Cleanly cut

III. Postmortem Lividity vs. True Bruise

Feature Postmortem Staining (Lividity) True Bruise (Contusion)
Mechanism Passive gravity distension of intact vessels Traumatic rupture and extravasation
Site Extensive over dependent areas At site of impact; anywhere on the body
Elevation & Margins Flat, no swelling; margins clearly defined Often swollen/edematous; margins diffuse/merge
Color Changes Uniform bluish-purple color throughout Dynamic color progression based on age
On Incision Blood is inside vessels, washes away easily; tissue pale Extravasated clotted blood, cannot wash; tissue deep red
Microscopy Intravascular red blood cells; no inflammation Extravascular red blood cells; inflammatory reaction

Pathology: Causes of Death from Wounds

  • Immediate Causes: Acute massive hemorrhage, reflex vagal inhibition, profound traumatic/neurogenic shock, or direct mechanical disruption of a vital organ.
  • Remote Causes: Systemic or localized secondary infection, gangrene/tissue necrosis, crush syndrome, surgical complications, thromboembolism, neglect, or the supervention of disease from a traumatic lesion.
