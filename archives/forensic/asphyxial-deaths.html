Asphyxia: Overview & Pathophysiology

  • Definition: Asphyxia is defined as a condition in which the availability, uptake, or utilization of oxygen (O2) is impaired.
  • Classification: The term asphyxia indicates the mode of death rather than the specific cause of death.

The Pathophysiology Vicious Cycle

Deficient oxygenation in lungs → Asphyxia
↓
Reduction in O2 tension → Capillary dilation
↓
Capillary stasis → Capillary engorgement
↓
Stasis of blood in organs → Diminished venous return to heart
↓
Reduced pulmonary flow → Loop repeats

Major Causes & Modes of Asphyxia

  1. Mechanical: Air passages blocked mechanically.
    • Closure of external respiratory orifices (nose/mouth) by hand or cloth (Smothering).
    • Closure of air passage by external pressure (Hanging, strangulation, throttling).
    • Closure of air passages by impaction of foreign bodies in larynx/pharynx (Choking).
    • Prevention of air entry into air passages (Drowning).
    • External compression of chest and abdominal wall interfering with respiratory movements (Traumatic asphyxia).
  2. Pathological: Entry of oxygen into the lung prevented by disease of the upper respiratory tract or lungs (e.g., bronchitis, laryngeal edema, spasm, tumors, abscess).
  3. Toxic: Poisonous substances prevent the use of oxygen.
    • Binding capacity of Hb with O2 reduced (CO poisoning).
    • Defective tissue utilization (Cyanides).
    • Respiratory paralysis (Opium, barbiturates, strychnine).
    • Paralysis of respiratory muscles (Gelsemium poisoning).
  4. Environmental: Insufficiency of O2 in inspired air (enclosed spaces, trapping in a disused trunk/refrigerator) or exposure to irritable/toxic gases (sewer gas, CO2, high altitude).
  5. Traumatic: Pulmonary thromboembolism (from femoral vein thrombosis due to lower limb injury), pulmonary fat embolism, pulmonary air embolism, or bilateral pneumothorax.
  6. Postural Asphyxia: Unconscious/stuporous patients due to heavy alcohol consumption.
  7. Iatrogenic: Anesthesia mishaps.

Clinical Progression Cascade

Asphyxia Setup → Decreased O2 tension & reduced Hb → Cyanosis
↓
Unconsciousness & loss of muscle power
↓
Capillary stasis, engorgement, and increased intracapillary pressure
↓
Capillary endothelium damage → Increased capillary permeability → Capillary failure
↓
Pulmonary Edema & Tardieu's spots creation
Note: Blood Fibrinolysis & Sphincter Relaxation
Asphyxia induces elevated venous pressure and blood fluidity via accelerated fibrinolysis, leading to the clinical presentation of cyanosis, visceral congestion, and sphincter relaxation (involuntary voiding of urine, stool, or semen).

Classification Frameworks

Classification I: Based on Defective Aeration of Blood (Gordon's)

  • Anoxic Hypoxia: Defect of oxygenation of blood in the lungs.
  • Anemic Hypoxia: Reduced O2-carrying capacity of blood due to low hemoglobin content or inactivation.
  • Stagnant Hypoxia: Inefficient or slowed circulation of blood to tissues.
  • Histotoxic Hypoxia: Depressed oxidative capacity of tissue cells.
    • Extracellular: Cytochrome oxidase enzyme system inhibition interferes with tissue oxidation (e.g., HCN poisoning).
    • Cellular: Oxygen is not utilized by cells due to interference in the dehydrogenase enzyme system (e.g., barbiturates, hypnotics).
    • Intracellular: Oxygen cannot enter cells due to decreased cell membrane permeability (e.g., chloroform, ether, halothane poisoning).
    • Metabolic condition: Uremia.
    • Substrate: Insufficient tissue substrate for metabolism (e.g., hypoglycemia).

Classification II: Medico-Legal Breakdown

  • Mechanical Asphyxia: Condition where external pressure is applied to the neck, chest, or other body areas, or the body is positioned such that respiration is physically restricted.
  • Non-Mechanical Asphyxia: Driven by toxic, pathological, or environmental physiological disturbances.
Tardieu's / Bayard's Spots:
• Round, dark-red, well-defined, pin-head sized petechial spots.
• Found where capillaries are least supported: conjunctiva, face, epiglottis, sub-pleural surface of lung, epicardium, and meninges.
• Mechanism: Raised venous pressure from impaired venous return causes overdistension and rupture of venules in lax tissues (not directly due to hypoxia of the vessel wall).
• Crucial Viva Fact: They are NOT pathognomonic of asphyxia; their absence does not exclude it. They are also seen in coronary thrombosis, anticoagulant therapy, and bleeding disorders (scurvy, leukemia, thrombocytopenia) with a more generalized distribution.

Stages of Mechanical Asphyxia

  1. Stage of Dyspnea: Breathlessness, feeling of oppression in the chest, heaviness in the head, ringing in the ears, tachycardia. Lasts about 1 minute.
  2. Stage of Convulsion: Loss of consciousness, reduced respiratory movements, facial congestion, bradycardia, hypertension, clouding of consciousness, and generalized convulsions. Lasts 1–2 minutes.
  3. Stage of Exhaustion & Respiratory Failure: Paralysis of respiratory centres, insensibility, flaccidity of muscles, loss of reflexes, and wide dilatation of pupils. Lasts 2–3 minutes.

Mechanical Asphyxia: Hanging

Hanging is a mode of violent asphyxial death caused by suspension of the body by a ligature which encircles the neck, where the constricting force is specifically the weight of the body itself.

Classification Typologies

  • By Degree of Suspension:
    • Complete Hanging: The body is completely suspended without any part touching the ground. Constricting force equals the full weight of the body.
    • Partial / Incomplete Hanging: The body is partially suspended; toes, feet, knees, or buttocks touch the ground (sitting, kneeling, or lying postures). Only a slight force (e.g., the weight of the head) is enough to cause fatal compression.
  • By Position of the Knot:
    • Typical Hanging: The knot is at the nape of the neck on the back, directly over the occiput.
    • Atypical Hanging: The knot of the ligature is located anywhere other than the occiput (e.g., side of the neck or beneath the chin).
Force Needed for Anatomical Compression:
Jugular veins: 2.5 kg • Carotid arteries: 5 kg • Trachea: 15 kg • Vertebral arteries: 30 kg.

Causes of Death & Fatal Period

  • Immediate Mechanical/Physiological Mechanisms:
    • Cerebral Syncope / Ischemia: Carotid artery obstruction cuts off cerebral blood supply.
    • Apoplexy / Venous Congestion: Jugular vein occlusion leads to a massive passive accumulation of blood in the brain.
    • Asphyxia: Compressive narrowing of the laryngeal and tracheal lumen, combined with an upward and backward displacement of the tongue blocking the pharynx.
    • Reflex Vagal Inhibition: Fatal pressure on the baroreceptors located in the carotid sinuses and carotid bodies.
    • Fracture/Dislocation of Cervical Vertebrae: Typically seen in judicial long-drop hanging.
    • Combined Asphyxia & Venous Congestion: The single most common presentation.
  • Delayed Secondary Fatalities: Aspiration pneumonia, edema of lungs/larynx, hypoxic encephalopathy, cerebral abscess, or systemic infection.
  • Fatal Period: Death typically takes place within 3 to 5 minutes (rapid asphyxia) up to 5 to 8 minutes of continuous suspension. It is instantaneous in cervical fracture-dislocations.

Autopsy Findings Summary

  1. External Findings:
    • Stretching and elongation of the neck; head tilted toward the side opposite the knot.
    • Face is swollen and cyanosed; eyeballs are prominent and staring due to passive venous accumulation.
    • Conjunctival petechiae; pupils are typically dilated.
    • La facie sympathetique: Occurs if the knot specifically compresses the cervical sympathetic chain, leaving the eye on that side open with a dilated pupil while the other side is closed.
    • Tongue is turgid, blue, and protruded out of the teeth with occasional marginal hemorrhages.
    • Dribbling of Saliva: Marks extending from the corner of the mouth opposite the knot, stimulated by pressure on the pterygopalatine ganglion before death. This is the surest sign of ante-mortem hanging.
    • Glove and stocking postmortem staining patterns on the extremities if suspended for hours.
  2. The Ligature Mark (Most Crucial Sign):
    • Site: Usually high up in the neck, above the level of the thyroid/hyoid bone.
    • Direction: Obliquely upward, backward, and non-continuous towards the point of suspension (creates a V-shape pattern).
    • Skin appearance: Depressed groove; initially pale but becomes yellowish-brown, dry, hard, and parchment-like over time due to friction ("rope burn").
  3. Internal Neck Dissection Findings (Bloodless Modified Y-Incision):
    • Subcutaneous tissues directly underneath the mark appear white, dry, firm, and glistening.
    • Platysma and sternocleidomastoid muscles may show localized hemorrhage or partial tearing.
    • Hyoid Bone Fracture: Occurs in 50–70% of individuals above 40 years of age. Typically breaks the greater horn at the junction of its outer $1/3$ and inner $2/3$.
    • Amussat's Sign: Transverse tearing of the carotid artery intima.
    • Simon's Sign: Hemorrhage on the ventral surface of the intervertebral discs beneath the anterior longitudinal ligament of the lumbar spine.

Judicial Hanging Mechanics

  • Conducted as part of legal capital punishment. The ligature loop places the knot strictly under the angle of the jaw or sub-mentally.
  • The drop length causes a violent jerk that induces a hangman's fracture: bilateral fractures of the pedicle/lamina of the arch of the 2nd, 3rd, or 4th cervical vertebrae.
  • Causes structural stretching or complete tearing of the cervical spinal cord, leading to immediate unconsciousness.

Mechanical Asphyxia: Strangulation

Strangulation is defined as a form of asphyxia caused by constriction of the neck air passages by an external force or ligature, without suspending the body. It is legally presumed homicidal unless explicitly proven otherwise.

Anatomical Variants & Definitions

  • Ligature Strangulation: Constriction of the neck using a cord, wire, clothing, or rope where the force is applied by an assailant.
  • Manual Strangulation (Throttling): Squeezing of the neck using human fingers, hands, or palms.
  • Mugging: Locking the victim's neck inside the bend of the elbow or knee from behind; external marks are rarely seen.
  • Bansdola: Squeezing the neck between two heavy parallel sticks or bamboos (one placed in front, one behind) tied forcefully at the ends.
  • Garroting: Throwing a ligature from behind without warning, which is then mechanically tightened using a twisting lever (stick or rod).

Autopsy Presentations (Ligature Strangulation)

  1. External Signatures: Face is profoundly bloated, congested, and deeply cyanosed. Tardieu's spots are far more abundant on the face, eyelids, and conjunctiva than in hanging. Eyes are prominent and wide open; foam or blood-stained fluid may escape from the mouth or nostrils.
  2. The Ligature Mark: Characterized by a horizontal, circular, continuous groove placed around the middle or lower neck, at or below the level of the thyroid cartilage. The base of the furrow is red, raw, and accompanied by distinct ecchymosed margins.
  3. Internal Neck Findings: Widespread bruising of subcutaneous tissue and neck muscle fibers. The hyoid bone fracture occurs rarely because the line of constriction is far below it, but thyroid cartilage fractures and tracheal ring fractures are common.
Pseudo-Strangulation Warning:
Natural skin folds in infants and children with short necks, tight buttoned collars on decomposing bodies, or heavy necklaces can leave grooves that mimic strangulation marks. Always inspect for true subcutaneous vital reactions and muscular bruising.

Manual Strangulation / Throttling Features

  • Driven by human hands, leading to rapid cerebral anoxia.
  • Autopsy Findings: Presents as distinct cluster bruising on the front and sides of the neck. Fingernail marks show as curved, crescentic abrasions. If one hand was used, expect a single thumb bruise on one side and multiple finger marks on the opposite side.
  • Hyoid Bone Fracture: Throttling is the primary cause of inward compression fractures of the hyoid bone, usually occurring within 1 cm from the tip of the greater horn.

Pathology of Hyoid Bone Fractures

  • Inward Compression Fracture: Seen in throttling. Squeezing forces the greater horns together, breaking the bone inwards. The posterior fragment is driven backward, and the outer periosteum is torn. Often bilateral.
  • Antero-Posterior Compression Fracture: Seen in hanging or motor vehicle run-overs. The bone is driven flat against the spine, expanding the divergence of the greater cornu, causing an outward fracture displacement. The inner periosteum is torn.
  • Avulsion / Traction Fracture: Results from extreme hyperextension of the neck, where downward traction from the thyrohyoid ligament tears the bone apart.

Suffocation, Choking & Café Coronary

Suffocation encompasses forms of mechanical asphyxia caused by atmospheric oxygen deprivation or physical obstruction of the respiratory pathways outside or within the body.

1. Smothering

  • Mechanical closure of external respiratory orifices (nose and mouth) by hands, plastic bags, pillows, or clothing.
  • Autopsy Findings: Abrasions and shallow bruises around the mouth and nostrils (though absent if soft pillows were used); inner lip lacerations or gum bruising from being pressed against the teeth.

2. Choking

Asphyxia caused by the physical impaction of an obstructive foreign object inside the internal air passages (e.g., coins, seeds, toys, blood clots, or the tongue falling back during an epileptic fit).

Penetration of Foreign Body into airway → Obstruction
↓
Inability to expel object → Stridor, respiratory distress, and loss of speech
↓
Deep gasping forces object downward → Intense Laryngospasm
↓
Vagal reflex stimulation → Arrhythmia & Cardiac Apnea → Death

3. Café Coronary & Creche Coronary

  • Café Coronary: Accidental choking where a large, unchewed food bolus impacts completely into the larynx. Typically seen in intoxicated individuals with a suppressed protective airway reflex. It mimics a sudden myocardial infarction because the individual collapses suddenly while eating. Treatment requires the Heimlich maneuver or emergency cricothyrotomy.
  • Creche Coronary: Identical accidental choking pathway occurring in young children (1–3 years) due to an underdeveloped dentition, weak cough reflexes, and a habit of placing random small items in the mouth.

4. Gagging & Overlaying

  • Gagging: Pushing a cloth or object deep into the oral cavity to block the pharynx, combining features of smothering and choking. Leaves tissue tearing in the back of the throat with blood seepage down the airway.
  • Overlaying / Compression Suffocation: Accidental crushing suffocation of an infant when a heavy nursing parent shares a bed and rolls over onto the child's chest and face during deep sleep.

Mechanical Asphyxia: Drowning

Drowning is a form of death caused by fluid aspiration into the pulmonary air passages following immersion or submersion in water or other liquid mediums.

Phases of Typical Drowning

  1. Voluntary Breath Holding: Retaining air until accumulating blood CO2 reaches the break point, driving an involuntary reflex to take deep gasping breaths.
  2. Phase of Struggle: Deep water inhalation accompanied by violent coughing, swallowing of water, vomiting, and progressive clouding of consciousness. Replaced air lowers the specific gravity of the body, causing it to sink.
  3. Convulsive/Unconscious Phase: Profound hypoxic convulsions followed by respiratory failure and cardiac standstill within a few minutes.

Pathophysiological Pathways

  • Fresh Water Drowning (Hypotonic): Water has low osmolarity (0.6% NaCl) relative to blood. Water rapidly passes across the semi-permeable alveolar membrane into the pulmonary circulation, causing massive hemodilution and hypervolemia. RBCs swell and rupture, triggering severe hyperkalemia. Overloaded heart mechanics and electrolyte imbalances trigger ventricular fibrillation within 4–5 minutes.
  • Sea Water Drowning (Hypertonic): Sea water salinity (3–4%) draws fluid out of the blood circulation into the alveoli via osmosis. This leads to profound hemoconcentration, hypovolemia, fulminant pulmonary edema, and rapid circulatory shock. Death occurs from myocardial anoxia within 8–10 minutes (without arrhythmias).

Atypical Drowning Syndromes

  • Dry Drowning: Occurs when a tiny droplet of water enters the larynx and triggers an intense, unyielding laryngeal spasm. No water reaches the lungs. Autopsy reveals dry lung fields, as death stems from reflex cardiac arrest or acute hypoxemia.
  • Immersion Syndrome (Hydrocution): Sudden cardiac arrest from vagal inhibition when an individual plunges feet-first into cold water. Triggered by cold water striking the epigastrium or entering the eardrums and nasal mucosa. Classical drowning stigmata are entirely absent. Alcohol intake increases risk due to generalized vasodilation.
  • Near Drowning (Secondary Drowning): The victim survives the initial immersion episode but passes away after 24 hours due to secondary complications like ARDS, chemical pneumonia, or hypoxic encephalopathy.

Autopsy Findings in Drowning Cases

  1. External Signatures:
    • Drowning Froth: Fine, copious, white, "shaving-lather" like foam at the mouth and nostrils. It is tenacious, composed of tiny bubbles, does not collapse when touched with a knife, and reappears after wiping. Formed by the agitation of surfactant, mucus, edema fluid, and aspirated water.
    • Washerwomen's Hand: Wrinkled, sodden, bleached appearance of the skin on palms and soles due to prolonged exposure to water (fingertips by 3–4 hours, whole hand by 24 hours).
    • Cutis Anserina (Goose Flesh): Puckered, granular skin surface caused by postmortem rigor mortis of the erector pilorum muscles.
    • Cadaveric Spasm: Grass, mud, weeds, or gravel gripped tightly in the hands. This is an absolute proof of ante-mortem immersion.
  2. Internal Signs:
    • Emphysema Aquosum: Voluminous, hyperinflated, boggy lungs that retain their shape and show rib indentations across their pleural surface. Slicing yields abundant frothy fluid.
    • Paltauf's Hemorrhages: Subpleural extravasations of blood resulting from alveolar wall ruptures.
    • Water in the Stomach: Presence of water in the stomach and small intestine indicates active ante-mortem peristalsis and swallowing.

Laboratory Validation Diagnostics

  • The Diatom Test: Diatoms are microscopic unicellular algae with acid-resistant silica skeletons (frustules). If a person is submerged alive, active circulation carries these diatoms from the lungs into the left heart, brain, and long bone marrow. Marrow from the femur is digested with concentrated nitric acid and centrifuged. Finding matched diatom species under microscopy provides robust proof of ante-mortem drowning.
  • Gettler Test: Measures blood chloride content in both chambers of the heart. A difference of 25 mg% or more is suggestive. Fresh water reduces left heart chloride via dilution; salt water increases left heart chloride via hemoconcentration.

Traumatic & Sexual Asphyxia

1. Traumatic Asphyxia

  • Results from mechanical fixation of the chest wall by massive unyielding objects (e.g., house collapse, crowd stampede, or vehicle roll-overs), making normal breathing movements impossible.
  • Autopsy Findings:
    • Masque Ecchymotique: Deep red-blue congestion and deep cyanosis of the face and neck, packed with dense petechial hemorrhages.
    • Demarcation Line: A sharp boundary line between the congested, discolored upper upper half of the body and the completely normal pale lower half. Driven by retrograde blood displacement from the superior vena cava.
    • Presents with rib/clavicle fractures, retinal hemorrhages (Purtscher's retinopathy), and severe visceral congestion.

2. Burking

  • A combined method of homicidal smothering and traumatic asphyxia, famously named after the serial killers Burke and Hare.
  • The assailant sits heavily on the chest of a helpless, often intoxicated victim to stop chest wall movement (traumatic asphyxia) while sealing the nose and mouth tightly with gloved hands (smothering). Leaves minimal external signs of violence.

3. Sexual Asphyxia (Autoerotic Asphyxia)

  • A paraphilia where sexual arousal and orgasm depend on self-induced, controlled hypoxia up to the brink of losing consciousness. Temporary cerebral ischemia from partial neck compression can trigger vivid erotic hallucinations.
  • The Classic Scene: Usually a young male found deceased in a locked room or basement. The person is often naked, dressed in female clothing (transvestism), and surrounded by pornographic materials or a mirror positioned to watch the act.
  • The Fatal Accident Mechanism: The self-release safety mechanism fails, causing sudden loss of consciousness followed by accidental death from hanging or hanging-associated vagal failure.
  • Key Forensic Identifiers: Soft padding or towels placed beneath the noose to avoid leaving permanent neck grooves, indicating previous practice and a clear intent to survive rather than a suicidal act.
